Neuropasticity and Growth Mindset

How understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people

Neuroplasticity is the capacity for our brain cells to change in response to our behavior. Neuroscientists used to think that the brain stopped developing in adolescence. We now know that the brain is malleable. This means that the brain is capable of adaptive change. Thus a person's ability to learn is not static. It can improve - or decrease - over time.

Neuroplasticity is such a transforming and empowering notion. Because it makes the hope of equality possible. It means intelligence is not determined at birth but is a journey that you are never to late to start.

Understanding neuroplasticity changes our attitude towards learning. Challenges become opportunities to grow and expand instead of moments of frustration and humiliation. We seek challenges and embrace them. The “what is the point” attitude is replaced with “OK I will give myself the opportunity to learn as much as I can from this”. Our attitude towards something really affects our ability to learn that thing. So having the right attitude is key to learning.

American psychologist Carol Dweck in her Ted Talk “The Power of Yet”, explains how neuroplasticity works and how we can implement its principle in schools. She explains that rewarding results is part of the tyranny of “now”. A “Fail” grade means that right now you don't have what it takes. In other words, right now you are not enough. That is why getting an F affects anyone's self-esteem. It makes you feel small, dumb, lost. But if you were to receive a “no yet” grade, instead of an F, your learning experience would be put into context: you are not there yet but you are on your path to get there. A "not yet" leaves the door open to the future, it points you towards the necessary steps that will take you to achieving your goal.

Dweck asks “How are we raising our children? Are we raising them for now instead of yet? Are we raising kids that are obsessed with getting As? Are we raising kids that don't know how to dream big dreams? Their biggest goal is getting the next A or the next test score? And are they carrying this need of constant validation with them into their future lives?

How can I engage with these principles for my own benefit

My whole life I have been resistant to try new activities because of my inner fear of not not getting it right immediately and looking dumb in front of others. Things that are meant to be recreational like bowling or minigolf or go-karting, can become a source of stress to me. How silly. Why would anyone expect me to get those activities - or any activity for that matter - right on the first go? And what does it mean if I do an awful job the first five times I try something new? It doesn't say anything about me. It only signals where I'm at in my learning curve. What a soothing concept.

Some of the ways you can increase your neuroplasticity

The best way to increase neuroplasticity is by exposing your brain to new things. This could mean trying to learn a new skill (like learning to play an instrument, or learning to paint). But you can do even simpler things like visiting new places, or changing the route to your usual locations. This forces your brain to create new connections, new bridges. The same happens when you expose yourself to different music. Another way is by practicing mindfulness. Intentionally journaling is also a great exercise and so is brain aerobics (using your alternate hand to do everyday activities like brushing your teeth or writing, or doing these activities while keeping your eyes closed).

Growth Mindste. Why is it relevant?

A grwoth mindset is a consequence of understanding neuroplasticity. It happens when we understand that our abiities can be developed. Dweck said "In a growth mindset, people believe that their most basic abilities can be developed through dedication and hard work—brains and talent are just the starting point. This view creates a love of learning and a resilience that is essential for great accomplishment. ...In the fixed mindset, everything is about the outcome. If you fail—or if you're not the best—it's all been wasted. The growth mindset allows people to value what they're doing regardless of the outcome. They're tackling problems, charting new courses, working on important issues. Maybe they haven't found the cure for cancer, but the search was deeply meaningful. ...When nurturing growth mindsets, it's important to acknowledge effort, but praise improvement."

In short a growth mindset makes the process of learning enjoyable, rewarding and effective.


In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?

Realizing how I have limited my ability to enjoy new experiences because of my fear of looking dumb was super enlightening. I realize now that these fears have little to do with shyness but are more of a consequence of growing up in a school system that praised results. Maybe I would be a different person if I had attended a school with a “not yet” mentality, that praises improvement regardless of the starting point.

This realization has prompted me to be more careful when I praise my own children. I've always wanted them to grow up with no fear of ridicule and failure but I wasn't quite sure how to ensure that. This exploration gave me some awesome tools that I can't wat to put into practice.


How will you integrate “growth mindset” into your learning journey?

Good question. I think I will be more compassionate towards myself. And I will work on my self-belief (or self-efficacy). Self-efficacy is a person's belief in their ability to complete tasks and to achieve their goals (Bandura, 1995). Judging yourself to be capable of success increases your chances of actual success. Judging yourself as not capable of success reduces the chance of success. A healthy balance between your confidence to undergo something, and your notion of what your strengths and weaknesses are will allow you to focus on how to overcome your weaknesses.
